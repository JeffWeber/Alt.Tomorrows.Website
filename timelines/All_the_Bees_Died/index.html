All the Bees Died

2031 — 2078

In early 2031, a silent RNA virus entered the global pollination trade through commercial honey bee colonies in the southern United States — asymptomatic in adults, lethal to larvae, undetected until it had already spread to every continent. Within six years, wild bee populations had collapsed by seventy to eighty percent across temperate regions, and the fruits, vegetables, nuts, and oilseeds supplying the world's vitamins had failed in two growing seasons. What followed was not the end of food but the end of cheap food — a decades-long project to rebuild pollination with machines, while the wild world quietly fell apart around the machines doing the work. By 2078, humanity feeds itself through technology. The question is whether it can ever stop.

The Divergence Era

The Years the Children Went Blind

2031 — 2035

The virus spread for six months before anyone noticed. It was perfectly designed for invisibility: infected adult bees lived full lifespans, showed no behavioral changes, and shed billions of viral particles through their ordinary work. Only the larvae died — eighty-five to ninety-five percent of developing brood, killed within days of exposure. By the time the first mass die-offs were documented, contaminated pollen had already reached every continent with active beekeeping. SDHI fungicides — used across most of modern agriculture — had quietly dismantled the antiviral defenses bees needed to fight back, reducing immune efficiency by forty to sixty percent. When wild solitary bees emerged from dormancy and fed on contaminated flowers, the virus crossed the species barrier with ease.

The caloric backbone of global agriculture — wheat, rice, corn, potatoes — held. Global calorie production dropped by only five to eight percent. But the nutrient picture was catastrophic in ways that calorie numbers couldn't capture. Over seventy percent of the world's plant-derived vitamin A came from pollinator-dependent crops. Ninety-eight percent of available vitamin C came from fruits requiring pollination. Within two growing seasons, produce prices spiked by three to five hundred percent. California's almond industry — six billion dollars annually — went to zero in a single season. In the developing world, the Micronutrient Famine arrived fast: childhood blindness surged, iron-deficiency anemia tripled in the worst-affected zones, and conservative estimates placed excess deaths at 1.4 million per year during the peak crisis years of 2033 to 2035.

Governments responded with caloric triage: emergency vitamin supplementation became the largest public health campaign in history, dwarfing pandemic-era vaccination drives. National stockpiles of synthetic vitamins became strategic reserves comparable to petroleum. Countries that manufactured them gained immediate geopolitical leverage. SDHI fungicides were banned worldwide by 2034 — but the virus had already become self-sustaining in environmental pollen reservoirs, and fewer than two hundred wild bee refugia survived globally.

  • 2031
    Silent Spread
    The virus circulates undetected through U.S. commercial colonies for approximately six months, entering international trade networks before the pathogen is identified.
  • 2032
    Cross-Species Jump Confirmed
    Researchers in Germany confirm the virus has jumped to bumble bees and multiple solitary bee genera. A joint team at Montana State University and the University of Würzburg identifies SDHI fungicides as the key enabler of cross-species transmission.
  • 2033
    WHO Nutritional Emergency
    The WHO declares a global nutritional emergency and begins emergency airlifts of synthetic supplements to Bangladesh, Mozambique, and Uganda, where vitamin A deficiency rates in children exceed fifty percent. California reports zero viable commercial almond harvest.
  • 2034
    The Bern Protocol
    One hundred and twelve nations sign an international ban on all SDHI fungicides. Bee populations have already crossed the functional extinction threshold in most managed agricultural landscapes.
  • 2035
    The Refugia Census
    A global survey identifies fewer than two hundred confirmed wild bee refugia — isolated pockets where small, partially virus-resistant populations persist. These sites are placed under immediate international protection.
  • Caloric triage governs all agricultural policy — staple grain expansion takes priority over all other food production.
  • Synthetic vitamin stockpiles are treated as strategic national reserves, equivalent to petroleum reserves.
  • International pollination trade ceases entirely; border controls on agricultural products tighten to quarantine levels.
  • SDHI fungicides are banned worldwide, though the virus is already self-sustaining in environmental pollen reservoirs.

The crisis exposed two simultaneous failures at once. Synthetic vitamin production could not scale fast enough to cover two billion people already below nutritional thresholds — not from lack of raw materials, but from lack of manufacturing capacity and cold-chain logistics. Meanwhile, the first generation of drone pollination systems completed field trials in California, the Netherlands, and Japan. These systems were crude and expensive, but they demonstrated that mechanized pollination of high-value crops was physically possible. The transition was driven by convergence: the proven inadequacy of supplementation alone, and the emergence of a technological alternative.

Epoch I

Reconstruction — Exhausting, Expensive, and Grimly Exhilarating

2035 — 2048

Governments treated pollination infrastructure the way previous generations had treated electrification or highway construction: as a fundamental public works project, whatever the cost. Drone systems evolved rapidly — from modified agricultural sprayers to purpose-built AI-guided fleets. By the early 2040s, micro-drones equipped with electrostatic tips and flower-detection algorithms achieved pollination rates between sixty and eighty-five percent for target crops. Lower than natural bee pollination, which typically exceeded ninety percent, but sufficient for commercial viability. Controlled-environment agriculture expanded in parallel: vertical farms, sealed greenhouses, and indoor growing facilities offered a structural solution by removing the pollination problem from the open environment entirely.

Crop genetics shifted fundamentally. Plant breeders launched emergency programs to develop self-pollinating or wind-pollinated varieties of previously bee-dependent crops. Some responded within a decade. Almonds and most melons proved far more resistant. "Pollen sovereignty" emerged as a foreign policy concept — the idea that a nation must control its own capacity to pollinate its food supply, independent of external technology providers. Energy costs became the binding constraint: vertical farms consumed ten to one hundred times more energy per kilogram of produce than pre-collapse open-field agriculture. The nations that expanded nuclear, solar, and wind energy fastest gained the greatest food security advantage.

  • 2036
    Tokyo Vertical Agriculture Act
    Japan mandates that twenty percent of urban commercial real estate be convertible to controlled-environment agriculture. Within five years, Tokyo alone operates over four hundred vertical farms.
  • 2038
    First Mechanized Almond Season
    A California cooperative completes the first fully mechanized almond pollination season using a fleet of twelve thousand AI-guided micro-drones, reaching fifty-five percent of pre-collapse yields — commercially viable for the first time.
  • 2041
    Independence Apple
    Cornell University releases the first commercially viable self-pollinating apple cultivar, branded "Independence" — consistent yields without any pollination assistance, though at some cost to sweetness and size.
  • 2043
    Singapore Enclosed Food System
    Singapore completes the world's first fully enclosed national food system, supplying seventy percent of the city-state's nutritional needs domestically — a model immediately adopted by island nations and city-states worldwide.
  • 2046
    European Pollination Grid
    The European Union establishes a continent-wide shared drone fleet managed by a central AI dispatch system, reducing per-hectare pollination costs by forty percent through economies of scale.
  • Nutritional reconstruction supersedes caloric triage as the governing agricultural priority.
  • Pollen sovereignty — the capacity to pollinate independently of external providers — becomes a core foreign policy objective.
  • Energy supply is the binding constraint on food security; nations that expand clean energy fastest gain the greatest nutritional advantage.

By the late 2040s, the technological systems that replaced bees had matured enough to restore most of the world's nutritional crop production — for populations wealthy enough to afford it. But the cost structure had fundamentally changed. Food that once grew with the free labor of wild pollinators now required continuous energy inputs, manufactured equipment, and centralized management. This crystallized a sharp new divide: nations with access to pollination infrastructure ate diversely; those without ate grains and supplements. Simultaneously, the dawning recognition that wild ecosystems — which received no equivalent technological rescue — were entering a second, deeper phase of collapse began to register as a political and moral problem that mechanization alone could never address.

Epoch II

Having Fixed the Problem, the World Realized the Fix Had Its Own Price

2048 — 2065

The food system hardened into two permanent tiers. The Mechanized Tier served roughly three billion people with restored nutritional diversity through technology-intensive agriculture. The Supplement Tier served the remaining five billion on grain-dominant diets augmented by synthetic vitamin programs and surviving bee-independent crops. The division tracked closely with pre-collapse wealth distribution, but was not identical — some middle-income nations that invested early in pollination infrastructure, including Vietnam, Morocco, and Colombia, outperformed wealthier nations that delayed. Wild ecosystems continued to deteriorate without fanfare. By 2055, flowering plant species richness had declined by approximately twenty to thirty percent in temperate regions. Fruiting trees and shrubs declined in wild landscapes. Birds and mammals that depended on wild fruit experienced population crashes.

Human biology began showing measurable drift in Supplement Tier populations despite supplementation programs. Vitamin A deficiency-related blindness became endemic in parts of South Asia and Central Africa. Average height in affected populations dropped by approximately one to two centimeters per generation — a quiet, invisible marker of chronic nutritional inadequacy during childhood. "Pollination debt" — the deferred maintenance cost of aging mechanized systems — became as familiar a fiscal concept as national debt. Nations that failed to maintain their drone fleets and vertical farm lighting saw rapid food quality regression. Meanwhile, the first virus-resistant bee populations began emerging from laboratory breeding programs, built from the partial-resistance lines preserved in the refugia. Reintroduction was painfully slow. Environmental viral reservoirs persisted, and each attempted return required a target region to be certified SDHI-free and virally suppressed.

  • 2050
    The Marrakech Accord
    One hundred and forty-three nations sign a binding framework for mechanized pollination technology transfer, funded by a per-calorie levy on Mechanized Tier food exports.
  • 2053
    First Successful Bee Reintroduction
    A joint Israeli-Australian team reintroduces a virus-resistant honey bee colony — bred from Negev Desert refugia survivors — to a sealed agricultural zone in Israel. The colony survives eighteen months before succumbing to a secondary pathogen. The proof of concept is real, but the fragility is sobering.
  • 2057
    UNEP Flowering Plant Assessment
    A UN Environment Programme assessment documents a twenty-six percent decline in global flowering plant species richness since 2031 and warns that further declines will accelerate as the plant-pollinator network continues to unravel.
  • 2060
    Svalbard Pollen Vault
    Norway expands the Svalbard Global Seed Vault to include a dedicated cryogenic pollen repository, preserving genetic material from over twelve thousand pollinator-dependent crop varieties.
  • 2063
    Eighteen Million
    The WHO estimates that cumulative excess mortality attributable to the Micronutrient Famine and its aftermath has reached eighteen million deaths since 2033, predominantly children under five and pregnant women.
  • Infrastructure maintenance is the governing priority — failure to reinvest in drone fleets, vertical farm lighting, and AI systems produces rapid food quality regression.
  • Pollination debt — deferred infrastructure maintenance — is tracked as a fiscal obligation equivalent to national debt.
  • Each bee reintroduction attempt requires target regions to be certified SDHI-free and virally suppressed before deployment.
  • The Mechanized Tier / Supplement Tier divide is the primary axis of global inequality, tracked and reported as a formal metric.

By the mid-2060s, the mechanized food system had reached maturity while simultaneously revealing its structural limits. Energy costs for controlled-environment agriculture consumed an ever-larger share of national budgets. Drone fleets required rare-earth metals and semiconductor components subject to supply chain constraints. The system worked, but it was expensive and fragile. Meanwhile, the first viable virus-resistant bee populations — bred in laboratory conditions from the partial-resistance lines preserved in refugia — began showing multi-year colony survival in pilot reintroduction sites. The transition to the present era was driven by the collision between escalating mechanical costs and the emerging, still-uncertain possibility of partial biological restoration.

Present Era

A System That Works, at a Cost That Rises Every Year

2065 — Present

The narrative of this era is anchored in the temperate agricultural belts of the Northern Hemisphere — California's Central Valley, the European agricultural lowlands, the North China Plain, the controlled-environment clusters of Japan and Singapore — with secondary focus on the Supplement Tier regions of South and Southeast Asia and sub-Saharan Africa, where the human cost has been, and continues to be, highest. Humanity has rebuilt its food system through technology. The replacement is more expensive, more fragile, and more unequal than the system it replaced, but it functions. Three billion people eat with restored nutritional diversity. Five billion eat grain, synthetic vitamins, and surviving bee-independent crops.

The central tension is a tradeoff between the proven reliability of the mechanical system — which works now, at known and rising cost — and the promise of biological restoration, which could eventually be cheaper and more resilient, but remains unproven at the scale that actually matters. Every resource spent maintaining the mechanical system is a resource not spent on bee reintroduction. Every failed reintroduction strengthens the argument for doubling down on machines. And underneath both debates, wild ecosystems continue their slow decline — a collapse that neither machines nor laboratory-bred bees can yet address, and that narrows the window for any biological solution with every passing year.

Governance

Most nations operate under modified versions of their pre-collapse political systems, but food security boards — appointed bodies that set pollination priorities, allocate vertical farm capacity, and manage supplement distribution — hold effective veto power over agricultural policy. In Mechanized Tier nations, these boards function as technocratic regulators. In Supplement Tier nations, they often function as the primary instrument of state authority, since control of supplement distribution is control of population survival. The Pollination Grid — now operational across thirty-eight nations — is managed by a centralized AI dispatch system that tracks bloom timing via satellite and dynamically allocates drone fleet resources based on crop priority rankings set by national food security boards.

Mindset

The average Mechanized Tier inhabitant operates with what psychologists now call managed abundance — the expectation that technology will continue to provide, contingent on infrastructure maintenance and energy supply. There is a pervasive low-grade awareness of system fragility that no previous generation experienced as a baseline: people understand their nutritional diversity depends on machines, not nature, and that machines break. On the Supplement Tier, the dominant mental model is managed deficiency — the expectation that survival is possible but nutritional completeness is not guaranteed. Supplement access is treated as a fundamental right but experienced as a dependency. Ambition is calibrated to the available resource base.

Tensions

The deepest structural disagreement is between Restorationists — who argue that resources should be concentrated on bee reintroduction and wild ecosystem recovery — and Mechanists — who argue that the mechanical system is proven, scalable, and should be expanded rather than supplemented by an uncertain biological gamble. Restorationists point to the ongoing wild ecosystem collapse: mechanical pollination only addresses agriculture, not the forty to fifty percent of temperate flowering plants that depend on bee pollination for reproduction, nor the broader cascades affecting birds, mammals, and insects. Mechanists point to the repeated failures of reintroduction programs. Within the Supplement Tier, there is growing resentment over the pace of technology transfer. A smaller but vocal movement — the Grain Purists — argues that humanity should accept the grain-and-supplement diet as the new normal and stop investing in the energy-intensive restoration of pre-collapse dietary diversity, framing mechanized pollination as an unsustainable luxury.

Cultural Norms

Food waste is treated with a severity that would be unrecognizable to pre-collapse societies. Discarding edible produce — particularly mechanically pollinated fruits and vegetables — carries social stigma comparable to public drunkenness. Beekeeping has become a quasi-sacred cultural activity. The remaining bee refugia and Biological Restoration Zones are treated as protected sites with the cultural weight of national parks or religious shrines. Images of bees appear on currency, public buildings, and children's educational materials with a frequency and reverence that reflects their lost ecological role. Nature photography and landscape painting have shifted from documentation to elegy — images of pre-collapse meadows in full bloom are a distinct genre. The "Full Plate" — a meal containing items from every available food group including mechanically pollinated produce — is a social event equivalent to a pre-collapse dinner party with expensive wine. Children's literature is saturated with bee stories; the dominant narrative archetype is the Return Story, in which bees are successfully brought back and the world blooms again.

A Day in the Life — Mechanized Tier, 2078

A Mechanized Tier urban resident wakes in an apartment adjacent to a vertical farm complex. Morning nutrition includes grain-based staples supplemented by greenhouse-grown leafy greens and a mechanically pollinated fruit — typically an apple, strawberry, or cherry from a limited selection of available cultivars. A daily multivitamin is standard. The commute passes through districts where vertical farm towers glow faintly from internal LED arrays. Employment in the food infrastructure sector — drone maintenance, vertical farm operations, pollen processing, AI system management — accounts for roughly fifteen percent of urban employment. Evening meals are grain- and vegetable-heavy, with small portions of mechanically pollinated produce. A piece of chocolate — unremarkable and affordable by Mechanized Tier standards, one of the few sweet indulgences requiring no pollination infrastructure — is still, quietly, a small luxury.

A Day in the Life — Supplement Tier, 2078

A Supplement Tier resident wakes in a settlement where the primary food infrastructure is a government distribution center. Morning nutrition is rice or corn porridge with a synthetic vitamin packet dissolved in water — retinol, ascorbic acid, folic acid, iron, and zinc. Fresh produce is rare and expensive when available. The daily schedule is organized around supplement distribution times and cooking fuel availability. Medical checkups for vitamin deficiency are routine — particularly eye exams for children and blood panels for pregnant women. The packet tastes, as many residents describe it, like nothing. Which, to those who remember fresh fruit, is precisely the point.

  • Food security boards hold veto power over agricultural policy in most nations, functioning as the effective center of state authority in Supplement Tier countries.
  • Mechanically pollinated produce is market-priced and largely unsubsidized; base nutrition — grains, legumes, surviving oils, synthetic supplements — is subsidized and price-controlled.
  • Biological Restoration Zones require SDHI-residue levels below detection thresholds and active antiviral treatment of soil and plant surfaces before any bee reintroduction is attempted.
  • The Pollination Grid allocates drone coverage by contract tier — premium contracts guarantee optimal bloom-window pollination; standard contracts receive best-available coverage; crops outside both tiers go unpollinated.
  • Flowering garden maintenance is a marker of surplus — growing ornamental flowers requires either mechanical pollination for seed production or resource-intensive vegetative propagation.

What Comes Next

Three variables are converging on critical thresholds. The energy cost of the mechanized food system is rising as infrastructure ages and climate change increases cooling demands for vertical farms; current projections indicate food-system energy consumption will reach fifteen percent of national grid capacity in Mechanized Tier nations by 2085. Wild ecosystem decline has not stabilized — flowering plant diversity continues dropping at approximately one to two percent per year in temperate regions, and the loss of plant species is beginning to cascade into insect, bird, and mammal population declines beyond the pollinator-specific losses; several temperate forest and grassland biomes are projected to cross into simplified, low-diversity stable states within twenty years, functionally irreversible on human timescales. Against both of these deteriorating trends, virus-resistant bee populations in three BRZ sites — Negev Desert, Tasmania, and central Portugal — are showing the first multi-generational colony growth without direct human intervention. The two timelines are converging: the mechanical system will hold, but at escalating cost; the biological system may recover, but only if it races the ongoing collapse of the ecological substrate it would operate within.

The World Without the Buzz

How temperate environments look, sound, and smell in a world where most pollinators are gone.

Acoustic

Outdoor environments in temperate regions are measurably quieter during warm months than any pre-collapse baseline. The ambient buzz of pollinating insects — once a ubiquitous component of warm-weather soundscapes — is absent from most landscapes. The dominant warm-weather sounds are wind through grass and foliage, reduced birdsong, and the low-frequency hum of drone pollination fleets during bloom season. Urban environments near vertical farms produce a constant low hum from HVAC systems, LED driver circuits, and hydroponic pump arrays, registering at roughly forty-five to fifty-five decibels — comparable to a quiet office. The silence where the insect sound used to be is one of the most commonly cited experiences in oral histories collected from adults old enough to remember the Before.

Optical

Natural landscapes display reduced color diversity. Meadows and roadsides show fewer flowering species; the dominant vegetation palette is green, brown, and muted yellow rather than the multi-spectrum bloom displays of pre-collapse temperate summers. Urban environments near vertical farms are characterized by the purple-pink glow of LED grow lights — heavy in red and blue wavelengths — visible through ventilation seams and loading bays. Nighttime skylines in Mechanized Tier cities include vertical farm towers glowing faintly with this distinctive spectrum. Daylight in open agricultural areas is periodically interrupted by drone pollination swarms — dark, coordinated clouds of small machines moving across blooming fields in algorithmically directed patterns.

Atmospheric

Agricultural regions carry less floral scent than any pre-collapse baseline. The complex bouquet of blooming wildflowers, fruit tree blossoms, and nectar-producing plants has been replaced by a simpler profile dominated by grass, soil, and grain pollen. Urban areas near vertical farms carry a distinctive mineral-and-chlorophyll smell — the off-gassing of hydroponic nutrient solutions and densely packed plant growth under artificial light. Ambient pesticide scent is largely absent due to the SDHI ban and the controlled-environment shift; air quality in agricultural zones has measurably improved in this specific dimension, even as everything else has narrowed.

Tactile / Thermal

The microclimate around vertical farm complexes runs slightly warmer due to waste heat from lighting and climate control systems. Humidity inside vertical farms is maintained at sixty to seventy-five percent — noticeably damp to workers entering from outside. The dominant textures of the food infrastructure are smooth metal, rigid plastic, and sealed concrete. Natural textures — soil, bark, leaf surfaces — are encountered less frequently in daily urban life than in any previous generation. Children raised near vertical farms report a notably different baseline for what "outside" looks like and feels like compared to pre-collapse generations.

What the Body Keeps

The measurable biological shifts in human populations and ecosystems after four decades without wild pollinators.

Human Baseline

Populations that have lived on the Supplement Tier for a full generation show measurable biological divergence from pre-collapse baselines. Average adult height in the most affected regions has decreased by approximately 1.5 to 2.5 centimeters compared to 2030 cohorts — a quiet, generational shrinkage invisible in any single life but legible in population data. Rates of xerophthalmia — vitamin A deficiency-related eye disease — have increased fifteen-fold in South Asia compared to pre-collapse rates. Folate deficiency has driven a measurable increase in neural tube defects in regions with inconsistent supplement access. Populations on the Mechanized Tier show no significant biological drift; dietary diversity has been largely restored through technology, though fewer cultivars of most fruits and vegetables are available than before the collapse.

Perception

The most measurable perceptual shift is olfactory and visual. Both tiers live in a world with fewer flowers, less ambient fragrance from blooming plants, and less color diversity in natural landscapes. Urban dwellers in Mechanized Tier nations spend significant time inside or adjacent to vertical farms, where the dominant sensory inputs are LED grow-light spectra heavy in red and blue wavelengths, the hum of climate control systems, and the mineral smell of hydroponic nutrient solutions. Children raised in these environments report a notably different baseline for what "outside" looks like and smells like compared to pre-collapse generations. Rural landscapes register as quieter — the ambient buzz of pollinating insects, once a ubiquitous component of warm-weather soundscapes, is largely absent. The silence where that sound used to be is one of the most commonly cited experiences in the oral histories collected from adults old enough to remember the Before.

Ecology

Wild ecosystems remain in active decline, concentrated most severely in temperate zones where bee pollination was dominant. Global flowering plant species richness has fallen by an estimated thirty percent since 2031. Fruiting trees and shrubs have declined in wild landscapes, reducing food availability for birds and mammals. Forest composition is shifting toward wind-pollinated species — oaks, pines, grasses — and away from pollinator-dependent flowering species. Some non-bee pollinators — butterflies, moths, hoverflies, bats — have partially compensated in tropical regions, but their populations are also stressed by the loss of plant diversity. Wild-gathered fruits and nuts are functionally unavailable. Staple grains remain open-field and unaffected, a fact that has shaped every political argument about the future.

Evolutionary Pressures

The remaining bee populations in Biological Restoration Zones descend from partial-resistance survivors identified in the 2035 Refugia Census. Intense artificial selection over three decades has amplified the partial-resistance trait — from five to fifteen percent pupal survival under viral load to sixty to seventy percent in controlled conditions — but has narrowed genetic diversity substantially. Reintroduced colonies show reduced foraging range and lower cold tolerance compared to pre-collapse bees, side effects of a severe population bottleneck. In wild plant populations, there is measurable selection pressure toward self-pollination and wind-pollination traits. Some formerly bee-dependent wildflower species are showing increased rates of autonomous self-fertilization in surviving populations — an evolutionary response, but one that reduces genetic diversity and long-term adaptive capacity.

Lexicon

The language of a world that lost something fundamental and built a vocabulary around the absence.

The Supplement
The daily synthetic vitamin packet that Supplement Tier residents dissolve in water and consume alongside grain staples. Contains retinol, ascorbic acid, folic acid, iron, and zinc. Replaces the concept of "a balanced meal" — what that phrase once implied about natural food diversity, The Supplement now attempts to replicate chemically. Residents often describe it as tasting "like nothing" — which, to those who remember fresh fruit, is precisely the point.
Emotional charge — Deep ambivalence. For many, survival and state care; for others, a daily reminder of what was lost and what is missing.
Full Plate
A meal that contains items from every available food group, including mechanically pollinated produce. A social ritual and status marker, equivalent in prestige and effort to a formal dinner party in a pre-collapse context. Replaces the casual abundance of a grocery-store world where building a varied meal required no special planning or expense.
Emotional charge — Pride, aspiration, and a layer of grief underneath both — the Full Plate is celebrated precisely because it is no longer ordinary.
The Buzz
The ambient sound of pollinating insects in warm weather, now absent from most outdoor environments. Used in everyday speech to describe not just the missing soundscape but the broader pre-collapse ecological fullness it represents: "before the Buzz went quiet," "back when there was still a Buzz." Older generations use it with a specific heaviness. Younger people who have never heard it use it abstractly, the way someone might say "before the war" about a conflict they only know from school.
Emotional charge — Grief, always.
Pollen Sovereignty
The political principle that a nation must control its own capacity to pollinate its food supply, independent of external technology providers or supply chains. The food-production equivalent of energy independence. Replaces the pre-collapse assumption that food security was primarily a question of land and water, not pollination logistics.
Emotional charge — Nationalistic pride in nations that have achieved it; bitter resentment in nations that have not. In Supplement Tier political discourse, lack of pollen sovereignty is framed as a form of structural colonialism.
Pollination Debt
The accumulated deferred maintenance cost of aging mechanized food infrastructure: drone fleets that need replacement, vertical farm lighting past its rated lifespan, AI systems running on outdated hardware. A fiscal and political concept as familiar as national debt. Replaces the pre-collapse assumption that nature's pollination services were free and self-renewing. Frequently used by Restorationists to argue that the mechanical system is not, in fact, free.
Emotional charge — Dread and low-grade resignation, particularly in middle-income nations facing their first major infrastructure replacement cycle.
Return Story
The dominant narrative archetype in children's literature and popular fiction: a tale in which bees are successfully restored and the world blooms again. Functions culturally as a paradise myth — an imagined restoration of a lost natural order. Beloved and contested at the same time: adults who have watched reintroduction programs fail repeatedly read Return Stories with a different feeling than children do.
Emotional charge — Hope mixed with suspicion.
BRZ
Biological Restoration Zone. A sealed agricultural perimeter where SDHI-residue levels have been reduced below detection thresholds and antiviral treatments are applied to soil and plant surfaces — the only environment where bee reintroduction is attempted. To residents near a BRZ, these zones are simultaneously scientific facilities and sacred spaces, the closest thing to a living pre-collapse ecosystem that most people will ever encounter.
Emotional charge — Reverence and anxiety in roughly equal measure.
Grain Purist
A member of the political movement that argues humanity should accept the grain-and-supplement diet as the permanent new normal and stop investing in the energy-intensive restoration of pre-collapse dietary diversity. Frames mechanized pollination as an unsustainable luxury and advocates for nutritional adequacy through fortification of staple crops. Their opponents hear the name as an insult; adherents wear it with a kind of grim pride.
Emotional charge — Contested. Deprivation or realism, depending entirely on who you ask.
