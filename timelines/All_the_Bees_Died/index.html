All The Bees Died

2031 — 2078

In 2031, a silent virus entered the global pollination trade and killed the brood of every bee it touched. Within six years, wild bee populations had collapsed by eighty percent. Humanity rebuilt its food system with drone fleets and vertical farms — a technological rescue that fed billions but divided the world into those who eat and those who swallow vitamins. By 2078, the machines work, the cost keeps rising, and the first lab-bred bees are surviving in sealed fields. The question is no longer whether we can feed ourselves. It is whether the living world that once did it for free can ever be reassembled.

Inciting Era

The Years the Children Went Blind

2031 — 2035

In early 2031, a hyper-mutated RNA virus emerged in commercial honey bee colonies across the southern United States. It was perfectly invisible — infected foragers lived full lifespans, showed no behavioral changes, and shed billions of viral particles through normal activity. But it was eighty-five to ninety-five percent lethal to bee pupae, killing developing brood within days. Because no adult bee ever looked sick, no alarm was raised. Commercial pollination contracts moved infected colonies across borders within weeks. By mid-2031, contaminated pollen had reached every continent with active beekeeping.

The virus's accelerant was already in the soil. SDHI fungicides — used across most of modern agriculture — had been quietly degrading bee immune defenses for years, reducing antiviral efficiency by forty to sixty percent. When wild solitary bees emerged from dormancy and fed on contaminated flowers, the virus jumped species. Within two years, commercial colonies had collapsed. Within six, wild populations had declined by seventy to eighty percent across temperate regions.

The caloric backbone of global agriculture — wheat, rice, corn — survived intact. But over seventy percent of the world's plant-derived vitamin A and ninety-eight percent of its vitamin C came from pollinator-dependent crops. Produce prices spiked three to five hundred percent. California's six-billion-dollar almond industry went to zero in a single season. In Southeast Asia, sub-Saharan Africa, and Central America, the Micronutrient Famine began. Childhood blindness surged. Iron-deficiency anemia in pregnant women tripled. At its peak, the famine killed an estimated 1.4 million people per year.

  • 2031
    Six Months of Silence
    The virus circulates through U.S. commercial colonies for half a year before any brood mortality is detected, allowing contaminated pollen to enter international trade unnoticed.
  • 2032
    Cross-Species Confirmation
    German researchers confirm the virus has jumped from honey bees to bumble bees and solitary genera. A joint Montana State-Wurzburg team identifies SDHI fungicides as the key enabler.
  • 2033
    WHO Nutritional Emergency
    The WHO declares a global nutritional emergency and airlifts synthetic supplements to Bangladesh, Mozambique, and Uganda, where childhood vitamin A deficiency exceeds fifty percent.
  • 2034
    The Bern Protocol
    One hundred and twelve nations ban all SDHI fungicides. Bee populations have already crossed the functional extinction threshold in most managed landscapes.
  • 2035
    The Refugia Census
    A global survey identifies fewer than two hundred wild bee refugia — isolated pockets in chemical-free habitats where small, partially virus-resistant populations persist. All are placed under international protection.
  • Caloric triage governs all agricultural policy. Grain expansion takes absolute priority.
  • Synthetic vitamin stockpiles are strategic reserves comparable to petroleum.
  • International pollination trade has ceased entirely. Border controls on agricultural products reach quarantine levels.
  • Countries that manufacture supplements gain immediate geopolitical leverage.
  • Emergency vitamin supplementation is the largest public health campaign in history.

Synthetic vitamin production could not scale fast enough — not from lack of raw materials, but from lack of manufacturing capacity and cold-chain logistics. Fermentation facilities required eighteen to thirty-six months to construct. Meanwhile, crude drone pollination systems completed field trials in California, the Netherlands, and Japan. They were expensive and imperfect, but they proved that mechanized pollination was physically possible. The transition was driven by convergence: the proven inadequacy of supplementation alone, and the emergence of a technological alternative that could actually put nutrients back on the plate.

The Mechanization Epoch

The Reconstruction Years

2035 — 2048

Governments treated pollination infrastructure the way previous generations had treated electrification — as a fundamental public works project, whatever the cost. Drone pollination systems evolved rapidly, from modified agricultural sprayers to purpose-built AI-guided fleets. By the early 2040s, micro-drones equipped with electrostatic tips and flower-detection algorithms achieved pollination rates between sixty and eighty-five percent for target crops. Lower than natural bees, which exceeded ninety percent, but sufficient for commercial viability.

Controlled-environment agriculture expanded in parallel. Vertical farms, sealed greenhouses, and indoor growing facilities offered a structural solution: crops could be mechanically pollinated or bred for self-pollination inside sealed systems. Plant breeders launched emergency programs to develop self-pollinating varieties of previously bee-dependent crops. Some responded well within a decade. Others — almonds and most melons — proved resistant. The concept of "pollen sovereignty" emerged: the principle that a nation must control its own capacity to pollinate its food supply, independent of external providers.

Energy became the binding constraint. Vertical farms consumed twenty to forty kilowatt-hours per kilogram of produce — ten to one hundred times more than pre-collapse open-field agriculture. The countries that expanded nuclear, solar, and wind energy fastest gained the greatest food security advantage.

  • 2036
    Tokyo Vertical Agriculture Act
    Japan mandates that twenty percent of urban commercial real estate be convertible to controlled-environment agriculture. Within five years, Tokyo alone operates over four hundred vertical farms.
  • 2038
    First Mechanized Almond Season
    A California cooperative completes the first fully mechanized almond pollination using twelve thousand AI-guided micro-drones, reaching fifty-five percent of pre-collapse yields.
  • 2041
    The Independence Apple
    Cornell University releases the first commercially viable self-pollinating apple cultivar. It sacrifices sweetness and size but produces consistent yields without pollination assistance.
  • 2043
    Singapore Closes the Loop
    Singapore completes the world's first fully enclosed national food system, supplying seventy percent of the city-state's nutritional needs domestically.
  • 2046
    The European Pollination Grid
    The EU establishes a continent-wide shared drone fleet managed by a central AI dispatch system, cutting per-hectare pollination costs by forty percent.
  • The governing priority shifts from caloric triage to nutritional reconstruction.
  • Nations compete for pollination technology, controlled-environment infrastructure, and self-pollinating crop genetics.
  • Energy cost is the binding constraint on food security. Cheap electricity equals diverse diets.
  • Pollen sovereignty is a foreign policy imperative on par with energy independence.

By the late 2040s, the technological systems that replaced bees had matured enough to restore most nutritional crop production — for populations wealthy enough to afford it. Food that once grew with the free labor of wild pollinators now required continuous energy inputs, manufactured equipment, and centralized management. A sharp new divide crystallized: nations with pollination infrastructure ate diversely; those without ate grains and swallowed supplements. And wild ecosystems — which received no technological rescue — were entering a second, deeper phase of collapse that mechanization alone could never address.

The Consolidation Epoch

The Fix and Its Price

2048 — 2065

The food system hardened into two permanent tiers. The Mechanized Tier served roughly three billion people with restored nutritional diversity through technology-intensive agriculture. The Supplement Tier served the remaining five billion on grain-dominant diets augmented by synthetic vitamins and surviving bee-independent crops. The division tracked closely with existing wealth distribution but was not identical — some middle-income nations that invested early, including Vietnam, Morocco, and Colombia, outperformed wealthier nations that delayed.

Wild ecosystems continued to deteriorate without fanfare. By 2055, flowering plant species richness had declined by twenty to thirty percent in temperate regions. Fruiting trees and shrubs vanished from wild landscapes. Birds and mammals that depended on wild fruit crashed. The visual character of the land itself changed — fewer wildflowers, less color in meadows and roadsides, a more uniform green-and-brown world.

Human biology began to drift. In Supplement Tier populations, vitamin A-related blindness became endemic in parts of South Asia and Central Africa. Average height dropped by one to two centimeters per generation — a quiet, invisible marker of chronic childhood malnutrition that no supplement packet fully corrected.

  • 2050
    The Marrakech Accord
    One hundred and forty-three nations sign a binding framework for mechanized pollination technology transfer, funded by a per-calorie levy on Mechanized Tier food exports.
  • 2053
    First Bee Reintroduction
    A joint Israeli-Australian team reintroduces a virus-resistant colony bred from Negev Desert survivors to a sealed zone in Israel. It survives eighteen months before succumbing to a secondary pathogen.
  • 2057
    The Unraveling Documented
    A UN assessment documents a twenty-six percent decline in global flowering plant species richness since 2031 and warns of accelerating collapse in the plant-pollinator network.
  • 2060
    Svalbard Pollen Vault
    Norway expands the Svalbard Global Seed Vault to include a cryogenic pollen repository preserving genetic material from over twelve thousand pollinator-dependent crop varieties.
  • 2063
    Eighteen Million Dead
    The WHO estimates cumulative excess mortality from the Micronutrient Famine and its aftermath has reached eighteen million since 2033 — predominantly children under five and pregnant women.
  • The governing priority is infrastructure maintenance and expansion. The mechanical system requires continuous reinvestment or it degrades.
  • Pollination debt — the deferred maintenance cost of aging drone fleets and vertical farms — is as familiar a fiscal concept as national debt.
  • Bee reintroduction is painfully slow. Each attempt requires SDHI-free, virally suppressed environments that take years to prepare.
  • Genetic research shifts from emergency cultivar development to long-term ecosystem re-engineering.

By the mid-2060s, the mechanical food system had reached maturity and simultaneously revealed its structural limits. Energy costs consumed an ever-larger share of national budgets. Drone fleets required rare-earth metals and semiconductors subject to supply chain constraints. The system worked, but it was expensive and fragile. Meanwhile, virus-resistant bees bred from refugia survivors began showing multi-year colony survival in pilot reintroduction sites. The present era was born from the collision between escalating mechanical costs and the emerging, still-uncertain possibility of partial biological restoration.

Present Era

The Dual System

2078

Humanity has rebuilt its food system through technology, but the replacement is more expensive, more fragile, and more unequal than what it replaced. The central tension is a tradeoff with no clean resolution: every resource spent maintaining the mechanical system is a resource not spent on bee reintroduction. Every failed reintroduction strengthens the argument for doubling down on machines. And underneath both debates, wild ecosystems continue their slow decline — a collapse that neither drones nor laboratory-bred bees can yet address, and that narrows the window for any biological solution with every passing year.

The Pollination Grid — now operational across thirty-eight nations — is a centralized AI dispatch system that coordinates mechanized pollination across entire agricultural regions. Access is tiered: premium contracts guarantee pollination within optimal bloom windows; standard contracts receive coverage on a best-available basis; crops outside both tiers go unpollinated. Forty-seven Biological Restoration Zones worldwide contain approximately thirty thousand managed colonies of virus-resistant bees. None has yet demonstrated self-sustaining population growth without ongoing human intervention.

Three variables are converging on critical thresholds. Energy costs are rising as infrastructure ages. Flowering plant diversity continues dropping at one to two percent per year. And in three BRZ sites — the Negev Desert, Tasmania, and central Portugal — virus-resistant bees are showing the first multi-generational colony growth without direct human intervention. The mechanical system will hold, but at escalating cost. The biological system may recover, but only if it outruns the ongoing collapse of the ecological substrate it would operate within.

  • 2078
    The Pollination Grid
    Thirty-eight nations operate under a centralized AI dispatch system coordinating drone pollination by satellite bloom-tracking and crop priority rankings set by national food security boards.
  • 2078
    Forty-Seven BRZs Active
    Forty-seven Biological Restoration Zones maintain sealed perimeters where SDHI-residue levels have been reduced below detection and antiviral treatments allow bee reintroduction attempts.
  • 2078
    First Self-Sustaining Colonies
    Virus-resistant bees in three BRZ sites show multi-generational colony growth without direct human intervention — the first sign that partial biological restoration may be possible.
  • Food security boards hold effective veto power over agricultural policy in most nations.
  • Mechanically pollinated produce costs three to eight times its pre-collapse equivalent. Almonds are a luxury good. Coffee is rationed.
  • Food-system energy consumption is projected to reach fifteen percent of national grid capacity by 2085.
  • Wild ecosystem decline has not stabilized. Several temperate biomes are projected to cross into simplified, low-diversity stable states within twenty years.
  • The two-tier system — Mechanized and Supplement — tracks roughly with pre-collapse wealth distribution but is not identical.
A Day in the Life — Mechanized Tier City, 2078

A Mechanized Tier urban resident wakes in an apartment adjacent to a vertical farm complex. Morning nutrition includes grain-based staples supplemented by greenhouse-grown leafy greens and a mechanically pollinated fruit — typically an apple, strawberry, or cherry from a limited selection of available cultivars. A daily multivitamin is standard. The commute passes through districts where vertical farm towers glow faintly from internal LED arrays. Employment in food infrastructure — drone maintenance, vertical farm operations, pollen processing, AI system management — accounts for roughly fifteen percent of urban jobs. Evening meals are grain- and vegetable-heavy, with small portions of mechanically pollinated produce. A piece of chocolate — unremarkable and affordable — is one of the few sweet indulgences requiring no pollination infrastructure and no ration.

A Day in the Life — Supplement Tier Settlement, 2078

A Supplement Tier resident wakes in a settlement where the primary food infrastructure is a government distribution center. Morning nutrition is rice or corn porridge with a synthetic vitamin packet dissolved in water — retinol, ascorbic acid, folic acid, iron, and zinc. Fresh produce is rare and expensive when available. The daily schedule is organized around supplement distribution times and cooking fuel availability. Medical checkups for vitamin deficiency are routine — eye exams for children and blood panels for pregnant women.

Governance

Most nations operate under modified versions of their pre-collapse political systems, but food security boards — appointed bodies that set pollination priorities, allocate vertical farm capacity, and manage supplement distribution — hold effective veto power over agricultural policy. In Mechanized Tier nations, these boards function as technocratic regulators. In Supplement Tier nations, they often function as the primary instrument of state authority, since control of supplement distribution is control of population survival.

Mindset

The average Mechanized Tier inhabitant operates with what psychologists call managed abundance — the expectation that technology will continue to provide, contingent on infrastructure maintenance and energy supply. There is a pervasive low-grade awareness of system fragility that no previous generation experienced as a baseline. On the Supplement Tier, the dominant mental model is managed deficiency — the expectation that survival is possible but nutritional completeness is not guaranteed. Supplement access is treated as a fundamental right but experienced as a dependency.

Cultural Norms

Food waste carries social stigma comparable to public drunkenness. Every part of pollinated crops is processed — apple cores, citrus peels, and berry stems become nutritional extracts rather than compost. Beekeeping has become a quasi-sacred cultural activity. The remaining bee refugia and BRZs are treated as protected sites with the cultural weight of national parks or religious shrines. Images of bees appear on currency, public buildings, and children's educational materials. Flower gardens have become markers of wealth — growing ornamental flowers requires mechanical pollination for seed production or vegetative propagation, both resource-intensive.

Tensions

The deepest structural disagreement is between Restorationists and Mechanists. Restorationists argue that resources should be concentrated on bee reintroduction and wild ecosystem recovery — pointing out that mechanical pollination only addresses agriculture, not the forty to fifty percent of temperate flowering plants that depend on bees, nor the broader cascades affecting birds, mammals, and insects. Mechanists argue that the mechanical system is proven and scalable, and that every dollar spent on BRZs is a dollar not spent extending the Pollination Grid to underserved nations. Within the Supplement Tier, growing resentment over the pace of technology transfer. A smaller movement — the Grain Purists — argues humanity should accept the grain-and-supplement diet as permanent and stop investing in energy-intensive restoration of pre-collapse dietary diversity.

Sensory Profile

What the world sounds, looks, smells, and feels like when the pollinators are gone.

Acoustic

Outdoor environments in temperate regions are measurably quieter during warm months. The ambient buzz of pollinating insects is absent from most landscapes. The dominant warm-weather sounds are wind through grass, reduced birdsong, and the low-frequency hum of drone pollination fleets during bloom season. Urban environments near vertical farms produce a constant hum from HVAC systems, LED driver circuits, and hydroponic pump arrays — roughly forty-five to fifty-five decibels, the volume of a quiet office.

Optical

Natural landscapes display reduced color diversity. Meadows and roadsides show fewer flowering species; the dominant palette is green, brown, and muted yellow rather than the multi-spectrum bloom displays of pre-collapse summers. Urban environments near vertical farms are characterized by the purple-pink glow of LED grow lights visible through ventilation seams and loading bays. Nighttime skylines include vertical farm towers glowing faintly in this distinctive spectrum. Daylight in open fields is periodically interrupted by drone pollination swarms — dark, coordinated clouds of small machines moving across blooming fields in algorithmically directed patterns.

Atmospheric

Agricultural regions carry less floral scent. The complex bouquet of blooming wildflowers and fruit tree blossoms has been replaced by a simpler profile dominated by grass, soil, and grain pollen. Urban areas near vertical farms carry a distinctive mineral-and-chlorophyll smell — the off-gassing of hydroponic nutrient solutions and densely packed plant growth under artificial light. Air quality in agricultural zones has measurably improved in one specific dimension: ambient pesticide scent is largely absent due to the SDHI ban and the controlled-environment shift.

Tactile and Thermal

The microclimate around vertical farm complexes runs slightly warmer from waste heat. Humidity inside the farms is maintained at sixty to seventy-five percent — noticeably damp to workers entering from outside. The dominant textures of the food infrastructure are smooth metal, rigid plastic, and sealed concrete. Natural textures — soil, bark, leaf surfaces — are encountered less frequently in daily urban life than in any previous generation.

Biological Reality

How bodies, ecosystems, and evolution have shifted in a world without pollinators.

Human Baseline

Supplement Tier populations show measurable biological divergence from pre-collapse baselines. Average adult height in the most affected regions has decreased by 1.5 to 2.5 centimeters compared to 2030 cohorts. Xerophthalmia — vitamin A deficiency-related eye disease — has increased fifteen-fold in South Asia. Folate deficiency has driven a measurable increase in neural tube defects in regions with inconsistent supplement access. Mechanized Tier populations show no significant drift, though dietary diversity remains narrower, with fewer available cultivars of most fruits and vegetables.

Ecology

Wild ecosystems remain in active decline, concentrated most severely in temperate zones. Flowering plant species richness has fallen by an estimated thirty percent since 2031. Forest composition is shifting toward wind-pollinated species — oaks, pines, grasses — and away from pollinator-dependent flowering species. Wild-gathered fruits and nuts are functionally unavailable. The food production system is approximately seventy percent controlled-environment and thirty percent mechanically pollinated open-field, by nutritional output. Staple grains remain open-field and unaffected.

Evolutionary Pressures

The remaining bees in BRZs descend from partial-resistance survivors. Thirty years of intense artificial selection has narrowed their genetic diversity substantially. Reintroduced colonies show reduced foraging range and lower cold tolerance — side effects of a severe bottleneck. Pupal survival under viral load has been amplified from five to fifteen percent to sixty to seventy percent in controlled conditions, but performance degrades where viral suppression is imperfect. In wild plant populations, some formerly bee-dependent species are showing increased rates of autonomous self-fertilization — an evolutionary response that reduces genetic diversity and long-term adaptive capacity.

Lexicon

The language of a world that learned to name what it lost.

The Supplement
The daily synthetic vitamin packet that Supplement Tier residents dissolve in water and consume alongside grain staples. Contains retinol, ascorbic acid, folic acid, iron, and zinc. Replaces the concept of "a balanced meal" — what that phrase once implied about natural food diversity, The Supplement now attempts to replicate chemically. Residents often describe it as tasting "like nothing" — which, to those who remember fresh fruit, is precisely the point.
Emotional charge — Deep ambivalence. For many, it represents survival and state care; for others, a daily reminder of what was lost.
Full Plate
A meal containing items from every available food group, including mechanically pollinated produce. A social ritual and status marker equivalent in prestige and effort to a formal dinner party in the pre-collapse world. Celebrated precisely because it is no longer ordinary.
Emotional charge — Pride, aspiration, and a layer of grief underneath both.
The Buzz
The ambient sound of pollinating insects in warm weather, now absent from most outdoor environments. Used in everyday speech to describe not just the missing soundscape but the broader pre-collapse ecological fullness it represents: "before the Buzz went quiet," "back when there was still a Buzz." Younger people who have never heard it use it abstractly, the way someone might say "before the war" about a conflict they only know from school.
Emotional charge — Grief, always. Older generations use it with a specific heaviness.
Pollen Sovereignty
The political principle that a nation must control its own capacity to pollinate its food supply, independent of external technology providers or supply chains. The food-production equivalent of energy independence. In Supplement Tier political discourse, lack of pollen sovereignty is framed as a form of structural colonialism.
Emotional charge — Nationalistic pride in nations that have achieved it; bitter resentment in nations that have not.
Pollination Debt
The accumulated deferred maintenance cost of aging mechanized food infrastructure: drone fleets that need replacement, vertical farm lighting past its rated lifespan, AI systems running on outdated hardware. A fiscal and political concept as familiar as national debt. Frequently used by Restorationists to argue that the mechanical system is not, in fact, free.
Emotional charge — Dread and low-grade resignation, particularly in middle-income nations facing their first major replacement cycle.
Return Story
The dominant narrative archetype in children's literature and popular fiction: a tale in which bees are successfully restored and the world blooms again. Functions culturally as a paradise myth — an imagined restoration of a lost natural order. The genre is beloved and contested at the same time.
Emotional charge — Hope mixed with suspicion. Adults who have watched reintroduction programs fail read these stories differently than children do.
BRZ
Biological Restoration Zone. A sealed agricultural perimeter where SDHI-residue levels have been reduced below detection thresholds and antiviral treatments are applied, making it the only environment where bee reintroduction is attempted. To nearby residents, these zones are simultaneously scientific facilities and sacred spaces — the closest thing to a living pre-collapse ecosystem that most people will ever encounter.
Emotional charge — Reverence and anxiety in roughly equal measure.
Grain Purist
A member of the political movement arguing that humanity should accept the grain-and-supplement diet as the permanent new normal and stop investing in energy-intensive restoration of pre-collapse dietary diversity. Frames mechanized pollination as an unsustainable luxury and advocates for nutritional adequacy through fortification of staple crops. Their opponents hear the name as an insult; adherents wear it with a kind of grim pride.
Emotional charge — Contested. A position framed not as deprivation but as realism.
