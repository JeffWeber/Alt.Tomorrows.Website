Mouth Moving Without Sound

When forgetting became a disorder, the cure returned her memories without the words.

May 16, 2026 2 min read Neurotechnology · Brain-Computer Interface · Psychological Thriller

The technician called it a "lacunar episode" the first time it happened, which made it sound worse than forgetting my sister's wedding anniversary. I nodded at the right moments and signed the intake form. Three weeks later I had a diagnosis: RLS-7, stage two, progressive. They gave me a folder with a blue ribbon on it.

My retrograde index was 0.34, which made me eligible for ReTrace.

The clinic smelled like warm plastic. The technician — Marisol, a small woman with reading glasses on a beaded chain — fitted the collar around my neck and told me to relax my jaw.

"You'll feel a kind of pressure," she said.

The first session returned a Tuesday in 2038. I was eleven, standing in my grandmother's kitchen while she cut oranges. I could smell the rind, sharp and clean. My grandmother said something to me and I laughed, but when I tried to hear the words again, they were gone. The memory kept replaying, the same eight seconds, my grandmother's mouth moving without sound.

I told Marisol after.

"That's pretty common," she said. "The emotional side tends to come back before the words do. That part usually follows."

Except it didn't. Six sessions in, I had forty-three recovered episodes and none of them had words. My sister called to ask how the therapy was going and I said fine, it's going fine, I just sometimes couldn't tell if what I was remembering had actually happened.

She said that was normal too. She'd read about it in an article.

I stopped scheduling sessions after that. My doctor sent two follow-up messages flagged as urgent. Unresolved RLS-7 can progress into significant cognitive disruption, the second one said.

I deleted them both. The oranges still come back sometimes, in the morning, before I'm fully awake. I lie there and let them.
