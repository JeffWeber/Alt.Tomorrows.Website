Sixty-Two Hours

A space elevator tech watches civilization burn and does the math.

May 15, 2026 2 min read Space Exploration · Survival · Dystopian

The first flash came at 14:22, station time, and I knew it wasn't a weather event.

I was 1,840 kilometers up the ribbon on a routine carbon-fiber inspection run when the south limb of Earth lit up. Not sunrise. At this altitude, sunrise is slow and pink. This was white and instantaneous. Then two more, spaced maybe eight hundred kilometers apart along the coast. I had no direct comm to ground; the microwave uplink had dropped eleven minutes earlier, diagnostic reading: FEED INTERRUPTED.

The climber has seventy-two hours of battery reserve. At full descent speed, 190 kilometers per hour, I was ten hours from the anchor platform in Ecuador. But I didn't know what Ecuador looked like right now.

I tried the orbital platform frequency instead. Port Apex, 35,982 kilometers up, answered on the second ping.

"Rowe copy," the duty officer said. "We're tracking twelve separate thermal signatures on the North American seaboard. You need to decide."

The cabin hummed at a frequency I'd only felt once before, during a ribbon resonance test three years ago. I looked down. The terminator was crossing the Atlantic, and the night side was wrong — too bright in places that didn't match any city grid I knew.

Descending meant ten hours into whatever that was. The ascent governor tops out at 550 kilometers per hour — sixty-two hours to Port Apex from my position. The ribbon was intact. I'd just done 400 kilometers of inspection.

I checked the radiation counter. The Van Allen flux was spiking — the detonations were pumping charged particles into the inner belt faster than any model I'd seen.

I sent the message: ASCENDING TO PORT APEX. ESTIMATE ARRIVAL 03:14 THURSDAY.

The reply came in four seconds: Copy. Bay three, starboard side. Light will be on.
