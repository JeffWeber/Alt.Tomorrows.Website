Mission Control had a protocol for anomalies. There were protocols for almost everything — for thruster misfires, for corrupted telemetry, for the death of a team member during a shift. What there was not a protocol for was a signal arriving late in a way that implied something had slowed it down.

Light does not slow down. This is among the more reliable facts in physics.

Dr. Yusuf Adebayo ran the calculation four times before he told anyone. He used two different clocks and had a colleague check his arithmetic with pencil and paper, the way they'd been taught in a world before the answer could come back in milliseconds. The nine-second discrepancy held.

"Interference," said mission director Carla Voss. "Solar weather, plasma, something in the transmission medium."

"We've seen solar interference. It introduces noise. It doesn't introduce consistent latency." Yusuf pulled up the waveform. The signal was clean — cleaner than expected, actually. "If anything, the signal quality improved."

The third message arrived. Seventy-three minutes, nine seconds.

The fourth: seventy-three minutes, nine seconds.

They published nothing. That was protocol too — you did not publish anomalies until you understood them, and you especially did not publish anomalies that sounded, to a headline writer, like something they might want to make into something else.

Yusuf spent three weeks eliminating explanations. Clock drift. Orbital calculation error. A firmware update on the probe's transmitter that might have introduced a processing delay. One by one the candidates fell. The nine seconds remained, punctual as a heartbeat.

He began, privately, to think about what else might cause consistent latency. There was a class of theoretical objects — dense, compact — whose gravitational influence on passing signals was well-documented. But the math required one to be positioned almost exactly between Earth and Titan, along a very specific corridor of space, and that seemed—

He stopped himself. This was how bad thinking started. You ruled out the likely, and then the unlikely started to look inevitable. That was not science. That was narrative.

In the end, the team found it: a firmware update, deployed six weeks earlier, that introduced a 9.3-second handshake delay in the backup communication array. The probe had switched to backup without flagging it. The delay was consistent. The signal was clean because backup had better shielding.

A mistake, caught and corrected. The protocols were updated.

Yusuf wrote up the incident report. He was thorough and precise and he did not mention the two days he had spent, quietly, doing other calculations. The ones he had deleted before anyone could see them.

He told himself it hadn't been real science anyway.

He kept the printout in a drawer at home, folded twice.
