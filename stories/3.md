The notebook had been kept continuously since 1963. That was the part that mattered most to Dr. Sasha Orimoto, more even than the data itself — the fact of the continuity, the chain of hands that had carried it. Field notebooks got wet and dried and stiffened. This one had been transcribed into a database in 1998, but the original still existed, held together by a rubber band that had probably been replaced a dozen times without anyone noting it.

Every spring, since 1963, someone had walked out to Sector 7 of the Whitmore Research Reserve and recorded the date of first bloom for Claytonia virginica — spring beauty, a small white wildflower, a reliable and early riser. It was, climatologists had come to understand, an excellent proxy. A living thermometer. Its blooms tracked the thermal accumulation of late winter with more sensitivity than most instruments.

The record showed a trend — gradual, then less gradual. In the 1960s, first bloom averaged April 3rd. By the 1980s, March 22nd. By 2010, March 7th. Last year: February 18th.

This January, Sasha had gone out on a routine equipment check. She had not been looking for blooms.

There were three of them, pale and small and entirely real, pushing through soil that had barely frozen.

She photographed them. She stood in the field for a long time. Then she walked back to the station and opened the database and looked at the column header: *First Bloom (date).* Below it, sixty-two years of April and March dates, then February, then — what? January 11th. A date that had no precedent, no neighbor, no context in the column above it.

She typed it in. The database accepted it without comment.

The thing about phenological records, she had always told students, was that they were optimistic documents. Every entry implied a next entry. Every "first bloom" assumed a following year in which the category would still make sense — in which there would still be a first bloom, a recognizable spring, a flower that knew what season it was supposed to be responding to.

She pulled up the sixty-two-year graph. The trend line, extended, suggested they had perhaps eight years before January blooms became typical. After that the models got uncertain. The plant could adapt — shift its trigger conditions, go dormant differently. Or it could mis-time its emergence repeatedly until it stopped emerging at all.

The notebook entry for that day was brief. She had written it by hand, the way the record had always been kept, even after the database: *Jan. 11. First bloom observed, Sector 7. Three individuals. Ground not frozen at surface. Air temp at observation: 6°C.*

She did not write what she had felt, standing there. Field notebooks were not for that.

But she kept the photograph on her desktop — three white flowers against dark January soil — and when people asked what she studied, she had started saying: memory. The memory of seasons. What ecosystems hold onto, and what they lose, and how you know the difference.
