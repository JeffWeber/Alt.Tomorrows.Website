I hurt myself on purpose today.

That's the part people don't understand when I tell the story. They hear "hurt" and they hear accident, hear failure, hear a body doing something wrong. But I'd been planning it for six weeks, ever since I got the rotation assignment to Daedalus Platform and realized I'd be working alongside a full-gain crew for the first time in my career.

I'm a structural integrity inspector. Hull seams, pressure bulkheads, the bracket systems on the ore handling arms. Twelve years of it, first on Ceres Station proper and then on the mid-belt platforms that the cooperatives farm out to contracted crews. I know my body the way I know a bad weld. I can read it. That's the whole point.

The full-gainers on Daedalus were belt workers who'd stripped their interpretive layer entirely, every last one of them. Going loud is what they call it. No color-coding, no urgency gradation, no amber bloom telling you how bad a shoulder tear actually is. Just the signal, raw, the way your great-great-grandmother would have felt it if she'd fallen off a scaffold. They did it as a statement. The cooperative controls the recalibration infrastructure, controls the node supply, and they wanted no part of anything the cooperative touched. I understood the politics. I did not, at the time, understand what it actually felt like.

Their crew lead was a woman named Katya Guillory, built like someone who'd been compressed by decades of heavy-grav work and had simply adapted. She didn't explain herself much. She watched me work for three days before she said a word.

"You check your dashboard before you decide," she told me. We were replacing a cracked bracket on Arm Seven, 40 degrees off the platform's rotational axis, boots mag-locked to the housing. "You feel something and then you go look at what it means before you know if you're scared."

I told her that was the point. That was the literacy. That was three hundred years of people dying from not knowing.

"Sure," she said, and went back to work.

She wasn't wrong to push. I thought about it for the rest of that shift.

The thing is, I'd read the history. I knew what unmediated pain had done to people, the way it used to flatten workers into uselessness, chronic conditions that ate people down to the bone before the Map existed. I believed in the system. I still do. But there's a version of the dashboard you can run where you've dimmed so far down that you're not reading your body, you're reading a report about your body. There's distance in that. I'd been living at about forty percent gain for years because forty percent kept me functional across twelve-hour inspection shifts without the accumulated strain building to anything I had to act on. My supervisor called it professional discipline. I called it what it was, which was that I'd learned to not quite feel myself working.

On the fourth week, during a rest day, I sat in my bunk and turned my gain all the way up.

I didn't go loud. I'm not a political person and I wasn't making a statement. I just wanted to know what I'd been dimming. I wanted to see the room.

It was not subtle. My left hip, which I'd thought was fine, lit up a sustained deep red I'd been filtering out for probably two years. Three fingers on my right hand were running a constant low amber from grip fatigue I'd attributed to the job and then ignored. My lower back was a story I hadn't been reading. None of it was catastrophic. None of it needed emergency attention. But it was real, and it was mine, and I hadn't been listening to any of it.

I sat there for a while.

Katya found me in the corridor before the next shift. I didn't say anything about what I'd done, but she looked at my face and seemed to know. She had that quality. She nodded once, the way you acknowledge someone who has just understood something you've known for a long time, and went past me without making it a thing.

I didn't convert. I kept my Calibration running and I kept the interpretive layer on because I'm an inspector and I work in environments where misreading damage can cascade into twenty people dead in a pressure event, and raw signal is not the right tool for that work. The cooperative is still extracting rents on recalibration infrastructure. The frontier settlements are still running drift-killed workers at rates the inner system calls acceptable. None of that changed.

But I run at full gain now. Eighty-five, ninety percent. I feel the hip. I feel the fingers. I scheduled the recalibration I'd let slide and the medic found early-stage node drift I hadn't noticed because I'd been dimmed down far enough that the gray at the edge of my vision hadn't registered.

The Map is only useful if you're willing to read it. That's not a complicated idea. It took me twelve years and four weeks alongside a crew of loud, deliberate, furious people to actually believe it.

I still hurt today, same as yesterday. The difference is now I know where.
